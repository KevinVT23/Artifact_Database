Rosetta Stone: An invaluable key to deciphering ancient Egyptian hieroglyphs, this granodiorite stele dates back to 196 BC and features inscriptions in three scripts: hieroglyphic, Demotic, and Ancient Greek.
Statue of Zeus: A magnificent ancient Greek statue created by the sculptor Phidias around 435 BC. It depicted the god Zeus and was considered one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World.
Great Sphinx: An iconic limestone statue near the Giza Pyramids, Egypt, representing a mythical creature with a lion's body and a human head, believed to be built around 2500 BC.
Nefertiti Bust: An exquisite painted limestone bust of the Egyptian Queen Nefertiti, dating to around 1345 BC, celebrated for its stunning depiction of the ancient queen.
Elgin Marbles: A collection of classical Greek marble sculptures, inscriptions, and architectural pieces that originally were part of the Parthenon and other buildings on the Acropolis of Athens, created around 447 BC.
Terracotta Army: An enormous collection of terracotta sculptures depicting the armies of Qin Shi Huang, the first Emperor of China, buried with him around 210 BC to protect him in the afterlife.
Tutankhamun’s Coffin: The ornate golden coffin of the young Pharaoh Tutankhamun, dating back to 1323 BC, discovered in his intact tomb in the Valley of the Kings.
The Pergamon Altar: An ancient Greek monumental construction built during the reign of King Eumenes II on the acropolis of Pergamon, Asia Minor around 150 BC, featuring a famous frieze.
Ishtar Gate: A grand gate part of the walls of Babylon, built under King Nebuchadnezzar II around 575 BC, adorned with glazed brick reliefs depicting dragons and bulls.
Code of Hammurabi Stele: An ancient Babylonian stele dating to around 1754 BC, inscribed with one of the oldest deciphered writings of significant length in the world, showcasing the code of laws created by King Hammurabi.
Mask of Agamemnon: A gold funeral mask discovered at the ancient Greek site of Mycenae, dated to around 1550 BC. It's thought to depict the legendary Greek leader Agamemnon, although this identification is debated.
Lion Man: An ancient ivory sculpture found in Germany, created around 38,000 BC. It depicts a humanoid figure with a lion's head and represents one of the earliest-known zoomorphic (animal-shaped) sculptures.
Venus of Willendorf: A small Paleolithic limestone statue of a female figure, dated to about 28,000 BC, found in Austria. It's notable for its exaggerated female features.
Lascaux Cave Paintings: A complex of caves in southwestern France famous for its Paleolithic cave paintings, estimated to be up to 20,000 years old, featuring large animals, human figures, and abstract signs.
Stonehenge Stones: A prehistoric monument in England, consisting of a ring of standing stones, each around 13 feet high. It's believed to have been constructed from 3000 BC to 2000 BC.
Moai Statues: Monolithic human figures carved by the Rapa Nui people on Easter Island in eastern Polynesia between the years 1250 and 1500 AD.
Cyrus Cylinder: An ancient clay cylinder, now broken into several fragments, from the 6th century BC. It's inscribed in Akkadian cuneiform script in the name of the Persian king Cyrus the Great.
Priam’s Treasure: A cache of gold and other artifacts discovered by Heinrich Schliemann in Troy, dating to around 2450 BC. The historical association with King Priam is controversial.
The Silver Caesars: A set of 12 silver-gilt standing cups, dating from the early 1st century AD, featuring figures of Roman emperors and allegorical figures.
Winged Victory of Samothrace: A Hellenistic sculpture of Nike (the Greek goddess of victory), created about 190 BC. It is admired for its depiction of movement and its rendering of drapery.
Dead Sea Scrolls: A collection of Jewish texts from the Second Temple period, discovered between 1947 and 1956 in the Qumran Caves near the Dead Sea. They include the oldest known surviving copies of biblical texts.
Golden Raft of El Dorado: A pre-Columbian gold votive raft sculpture made by the Muisca people of Colombia. The piece is a key icon of the El Dorado legend, dating back to approximately 600 AD.
Antikythera Mechanism: An ancient Greek analogue computer used to predict astronomical positions and eclipses for calendrical and astrological purposes, dated to about 87 BC.
Portland Vase: A Roman cameo glass vase, which is believed to date to the reign of Augustus (around 25 BC), famous for its beautifully detailed depictions of classical figures.
Lothair Crystal: A 9th-century carved crystal disk, one of the finest surviving Carolingian engraved gems, depicting the biblical story of Susanna.
Book of Kells: An illuminated manuscript Gospel book in Latin, containing the four Gospels of the New Testament together with various prefatory texts and tables, created by Celtic monks around 800 AD.
Bayeux Tapestry: An embroidered cloth nearly 70 meters long and 50 centimeters tall, which depicts the events leading up to the Norman conquest of England concerning William, Duke of Normandy, and Harold, Earl of Wessex, later King of England, and culminating in the Battle of Hastings.
Sutton Hoo Helmet: An ornate Anglo-Saxon helmet found in the Sutton Hoo ship burial, dating to the early 7th century AD. It is a seminal piece of Anglo-Saxon art.
Lewis Chessmen: A group of distinctive 12th-century chess pieces, carved from walrus ivory and whales' teeth, found on the Isle of Lewis in the Outer Hebrides, Scotland.
Lindow Man: A naturally-preserved bog body of a man discovered in a peat bog at Lindow Moss, Cheshire, England, dating to around the 1st century AD.
Durer’s Rhinoceros: A woodcut of a rhinoceros by the German painter and printmaker Albrecht Dürer in 1515. It is one of the most famous depictions of an animal in European art history.
David Statue: A masterpiece of Renaissance sculpture created by Michelangelo from 1501 to 1504, representing the biblical hero David, a favored subject in the art of Florence.
Golden Hind Figurehead: The figurehead from the Golden Hind, the English galleon best known for its global circumnavigation between 1577 and 1580, captained by Sir Francis Drake.
Shroud of Turin: A length of linen cloth bearing the negative image of a man, which some believe to be the burial shroud of Jesus of Nazareth. Radiocarbon dating places its origin in the medieval period, around 1350 AD.
Amber Room Panels: Part of a world-famous chamber decorated in amber panels backed with gold leaf and mirrors, located in the Catherine Palace of Tsarskoye Selo near Saint Petersburg. Originally constructed in the 18th century in Prussia, the Amber Room was a masterpiece of baroque art.
Piri Reis Map: A world map compiled in 1513 by the Ottoman admiral and cartographer Piri Reis. Much of the map's notoriety comes from the depiction of parts of the American continent, which Piri Reis claimed to have used Columbus's maps, now lost, as a source.
Voynich Manuscript: A mysterious illustrated codex hand-written in an unknown writing system, possibly dating to the early 15th century. The manuscript is named after Wilfrid Voynich, a Polish-Samogitian book dealer who acquired it in 1912.
Magna Carta: A charter of rights agreed to by King John of England at Runnymede, near Windsor, on 15 June 1215. It is a foundational text in English and American constitutional history.
Ahnenerbe Chalice: A ceremonial chalice associated with the Ahnenerbe organization of Nazi Germany, created around 1935. The Ahnenerbe was an institute in Nazi Germany purposed to research the archaeological and cultural history of the Aryan race.
Imperial Seal of China: Also known as the Heirloom Seal of the Realm, this was a Chinese jade seal carved during the Warring States period (475–221 BC) and was historically passed down through generations of Chinese emperors.
The Buddha of Bamiyan: statues were monumental examples of Gandhāran art in the Bamyan Valley of Afghanistan, carved into a cliffside. Dating back to 507 AD, these colossal figures represented a fusion of Indian Buddhist iconography with the classical style of Hellenistic art.
Tang Dynasty Horse: This exquisite ceramic horse hails from the Tang Dynasty period of China, dating back to between the 7th and 10th centuries AD. Renowned for its lifelike representation and dynamic posture, the sculpture reflects the high level of craftsmanship and artistry achieved during this era. The horse, often depicted in full gallop with one leg raised, symbolizes the importance of the horse in Tang society, not only as a means of transportation and military power but also as a symbol of wealth and status. The use of rich glazes and detailed adornments on these sculptures exemplifies the Tang Dynasty's advancements in ceramic technology and aesthetic sophistication.
The Palermo Stone: is an important Egyptian archaeological find, dating back to around 2350 BC. This slab features some of the earliest known hieroglyphic inscriptions about the pharaohs and the annual events of their reigns, offering invaluable insights into ancient Egyptian history.
Disc of Festos: Discovered in the Minoan palace of Phaistos, the Disc of Festos, dating to approximately 1850 BC, is a unique artifact. This mysterious clay disk is inscribed with a spiral of stamped symbols, whose purpose and meaning remain a topic of debate among archaeologists.
The Euphronios Krater:Masterpiece of ancient Greek vase painting from around 515 BC, is renowned for its detailed depictions of Homeric legends. This red-figure vase, attributed to the painter Euphronios, is an exceptional example of the artistry of classical Athens.
Dragon Age Origins: is a landmark video game developed in the United States and released in 2009. As a role-playing game, it set new standards in narrative depth and world-building, influencing the genre significantly.
The Black Lotus card: from the 1993 United States Magic: The Gathering card game, is one of the most iconic and valuable trading cards in gaming history. Known for its rarity and powerful effect, it holds a legendary status among collectors and players.
The Blue Eyes White Dragon: is a fictional card from the "Yu-Gi-Oh!" trading card and media franchise, originating from Japan. The card, known for its powerful in-game attributes and iconic status, has become a cultural symbol in the world of trading card games.
The Triforce: a fictional artifact from Nintendo's "The Legend of Zelda" series, is a symbol of Japanese video game culture. Introduced in 1986 and evolving through the series, it represents a quest for balance and heroism in a mythic land.
The Dendera Zodiac: an ancient bas-relief from Egypt dating back to around 50 BC, is one of the earliest known detailed representations of the celestial sky and zodiac. Originally from the ceiling of the Dendera Temple complex, it is a key artifact in understanding ancient Egyptian astronomy and astrology.
